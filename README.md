## Overview

The griffin (also gryphon) is one of the oldest hybrid creatures in western mythology. It has the body, hindquarters, and tail of a lion and the head, wings, and (often) the forelimb-talons of an eagle, combining the 'king of beasts' and 'king of birds'. Griffin-like creatures appear in cylinder seals from Mesopotamia c. 3000 BC and on a cosmetic palette from Hierakonpolis dating to 3300–3100 BC; griffin-types feature prominently in Minoan art at Knossos c. 1650–1600 BC and across the Bronze Age Levant. In Greek tradition, beginning with the 7th-century-BC traveller Aristeas of Proconnesus and amplified by Aeschylus, Herodotus, and Pliny the Elder, griffins were said to guard hoards of gold in the deserts of Central Asia and to fight the one-eyed Arimaspi people. In medieval Christian Europe the griffin became a symbol of Christ's dual divine and earthly nature, and from the late Middle Ages it is one of the most common heraldic charges. In 2000 the classical folklorist Adrienne Mayor proposed that ancient griffin lore was inspired by the Protoceratops fossils observed by Scythian nomadic prospectors in the gold-bearing Tien Shan and Altai regions; her hypothesis remains debated, with palaeontologist Mark P. Witton arguing that griffin anatomy is plainly that of lion and eagle composites.

## Theories & Explanations

### Scientific

**Protoceratops fossil hypothesis (Mayor 2000)** _(Adrienne Mayor)_

Adrienne Mayor proposed that Scythian nomads prospecting for gold in the Tien Shan and Altai mountains observed beaked Protoceratops fossils and reported them to Greek travellers, generating the Greek griffin tradition.

**Composite-art hypothesis (Witton)** _(Mark P. Witton)_

Palaeontologist Mark P. Witton argues that griffin imagery is plainly that of an eagle-lion composite developed within ancient art traditions, with no need to posit dinosaur fossil inspiration.

### Skeptical

**Folkloric and heraldic creature** _(mainstream classical scholarship)_

Mainstream classical scholarship treats the griffin as a creature of art and myth, never historically claimed to physically exist.
